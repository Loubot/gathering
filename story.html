A story of the Eagle and a bird

In a deep dark forest an ancient people with strange customs lived. There was a river running through their part of the forest and when their children reached a certain age they were separated by gender with the boys going to one side of the river and the girls going to the other.

On the side that was for the boys the men gather together at night around the communal fire and told stories and smoked their pipes. Each family had its own special blend of tobacco, which has kept within the family. They also had a second blend that each family was happy to share with their neighbours. The custom of sharing pipers within the family and presenting a different blend to those out-side the family was a well-established tradition. No one quite knew where it came from. The stories they told were, all in all, much the same between the families though there was about 100 different ones. The stories were all held in common more or less. The most common, though least understood story, was a strange story of a Great eagle that once flew the land, fought and overcame the great dragon, before this great bird was finally killed trying to save his friends. There was a lot more to the story but that seemed to be the general thread to it.

On the side of the river that was for the girls much the same thing happened, but in a much different way. Again the women gathered around their fire and told stories - the same ones that the lads told but from a different perspective. I neglected to mention that on the men's side they told other stories just like the women of the day's work and of their daily struggles keeping the forest back and their clearing clear. Instead of smoking a special blend of tobacco the women made traditional dresses. The intricate stitches used were kept within each family and passed on from generation to generation.

When a young couple start to be aware of each other and decided that they wanted to be together. The community as a whole - that's both the men and the women come together to build the couple a raft. They sang - "We all want to give them the best start in life possible" as a work shanty song to help them work together while they built the raft.

When the time came for the young couple to come together for their first night there is a great celebration. And as they leave for the raft each of the parents give a parting gift: the boys get the special blend of tobacco and its secret and the girls get the traditional dresses and the secret of the special family stitch. Their parents assure them that these generational gifts will be a great comfort to them when the going gets tough.

Most of the young couples setting out on life were so in love that they usually didn't open their family gifts for a goodly length of time. As they moved down the river, going in the most part with the flow, they started to notice campfires doted along either side of the river. They always appeared in twos - one on the left and one on the right sides of the river. It reminded them of home and they felt a strange tug from these fires though they didn't speak of it, the tug for the young husband was for the fires on the right and for the wife it was the fires on the left. But as long as they didn't open their special family gifts they would freely kept moving along - further and further down the river. They, from time to time, remembered and told each other the stories they grew up with around their fires back home. They noticed that though their stories were different they were the same in many ways. But the story that had the greatest draw on them and seemed to flow with the river was an older story - the story of the great eagle. The reasoned why this story grew in importance for them is that they started to remember an important part of the story that they had until that point in their journey forgotten. They remembered that in the story the great eagle spoke of a journey like their one with a broad lake at the end of it. And as they moved on in harmony with the river's music a hope of leaving the river and joining a great broad lake began to grow in them also. They started to hope that they might be able to start a new life of their own by travelling to this lake and living not separate lives at their own campfires but together on this lake’s shore.

Then the ruff water came. They knew it would, as that was a part of all the stories they were told at home. At the start they were determined that they would just keep going listening not so much to the mad rush of the ruff surface water, but rather to the deeper note below. After each hard day they were able to find calm water to stop for the night. This calm water was well lit from the many campfires on either shore. After a number of nights in these calm places it slowly became apparent to them that this is where most couples ended their journeys and left the river. 

They determined to continue on, despite the growing sense of fear they were each experiencing. As a way of their coping they each would take out their comforter but without the other noticing. The husband started to mix his special blend of tobacco and she would admire the fine stitching on her now prized procession. They kept travelling on though the tug of the communal campfires was growing in each of them, yet they still had the hope of coming to a broad lake were they could be free of the river and the tug of the campfires. Imagine their horror at the end of this day to see the forest on either side of the river close in to meet in the middle. In fear they stopped. They weighted anchor in mid-river.

As the days and the years went by, they found themselves visiting the inviting fires on either side of the river. Each night they sat around the fires with their special tobacco and traditional dress, he on his side and she on hers. They hadn't quite grown apart yet though they were slowly becoming like their parents they left behind and the ancient people of old: her with her dress and special stitching made her welcome at the women's campfire and he with his special blend of tobacco at the men's. They missed their life on the raft and their shared hopes so they always tried to get back to sleep on their raft each night. They lay quietly side by side, anchored and trapped in their fear and by the pull of old life. Yet they were not just ready to give up on their hopes and dreams. And when they retold the story of the eagle this seemed to help in some way.

One morning the sun rose brightly. And in a bird flew directly to them. Strangely this happened as they had been retelling the eagle story again. And at first they thought it was the eagle coming to encourage them or better to tell them the way to the lake. As it settled on the front of the raft it sang an ancient bird-song re-telling the story of an eagle that had flown through the darkest darkness to the broad-shored lake. The dove stopped its song for just long enough to say, "come follow me". They started to tell this little bird why it was no longer possible to travel on - they told how they had lost their youthful fire; and that they were becoming more and more like the ancients on the banks of the river. But most of all they spoke of not being able to see away forward through the forest that was blocking their way. But all that the dove said in its soft still voice was "come follow me". They reasoned - this was no mighty eagle but a little gentle dove. Who would follow a gentle dove?
